Since the ascension of LLMs 3 years ago, major changes occurred in the various disciples of computer science. Only handful of people write code daily without relying on AI, and most of engineers today became agent therapists rather the craftsmen passing hours polishing their "creation".

This short blog is not about evaluating this phenomenon, but for the build up of an idea that rely on the answer to a specific question :
**"Why was computer science one of the first jobs to be automated?"**

### The "finite" machine 

Mainly; it is because "Computers are bounded", or in other word a  predictable and a finite machine. Memory is finite, storage is finite, and  even the randomness is not real. and this make up for many things:

1. Data that you can gather about a computer-based phenomena is often "accurate" as it is usually possible to cover from all "the angles".
for instance, a program context must be finite and "known" before execution. The variables, outputs, inputs, and the behaviour of the program whether it is true or faulty can be described with a considerable degree of accuracy, even if it is "tedious" and "too detailed". It may take 40000 pages to describe a software, but it can be "formulated" because it is a 0-1 in the end.
This is very hard for human interactions that involve **unknown** variables and an unpredictable events. For example, often people fail to explain why did their emotions changed in regard to one person, or their "hunch" about an answer in an exam. Not because they lake more "data" or "insight" about their lives but because these emotions and thoughts are not yet produced by a mechanism that we understand, but rather we see their effects on the brain and the hormones 

2. Because we have visibility about the extent of this machine and its output, the paths that to be taken can be **enumerated** and **analysed** with high precision.
In other words, AI can find the best outcome in the digital maze because they are of the same "finite" nature. this make it logical to understand the high quality of code (that is today hard to distinguish from the output of a senior engineer) comparable to the laughable slop that you will get if you asked a chatbot to write a poem, as the latter condense an experience and ideas that the AI cannot contextualize by definition.

But AI is still a human product that mirrors the human experience, or in the other words : Analogies of reality can work well on AI if defined within its own constraints and "logic".

Now, let's switch to the next thread : 


###  War

War is -by far- the most hectic and unpredictable experience in the human interaction. Weather, moral, surprise events, "strike of luck" and "genius" all makes it an impossible task to dominate it and condense it in a chart and some representative data, let alone expecting a plan to work at perfection. The rule in these events has been accurately described by the US. General -later president- Eisenhower as he said :"Plans are worthless, but planning is everything", insinuating that no plan works as expected, but the idea of knowing where to go and what to expect initially does make a difference (a big one).
Despite that, many "actions" and "reactions" in a military operation are repeatable. These "patterns" has been extracted to make **training scenarios** on the tactical level, but on the "cognitive" operational and strategical level, training is quiet different (and sometimes not enough, even for some tactics). Also, there are situations that cannot be part of training, for instance : what to do if the enemy holds a hostage ? what to do if the enemy encircle an important building ? what to do to when the weather changes suddenly?
To cover these hypothesis (which can be enumerated but not simulated), and the operational/strategical patterns, "Military Doctrines" has been put into place as how-to act generally during operations and in various situation, what are the rules the rules of engagement, and how should units proceed.

A doctrine is a playbook, not a "must do" list. It should be the naturel reaction to an event, but it can change if the soldier/leader judges it necessary (which require a rational that exists rarely under a torrent of bullets);

### The Intersection: a doctrine for agentic "soldiers" 

We are in a state of  "unlimited warfare", seen in the ever growing number of cyber attacks targeting anything of any value that dares peeking off the net. Everyone is on this battlefield. And just like computer science knew this massive shake; Cyber Security changed considerably recently with the rise of agents as they are taking part to these wars as attackers and defenders and getting better at it, to a point where many complex campaigns were conducted using autonomous workflow (as Anthropic disclosed recently about disturbing a campaign using Claude capabilities), or to discover and exploit complex vulnerabilities that no human would have thought off. (why they would not? because a human will not explore every single file looking for a vulnerable line, and enumerate every possibility that code can be used for.)

But these agents are lead and orchestrated as if they are workers in a factory and not soldiers, either because engineers are too focused on the technical details that AI may be aware off it better then they do or on the scoping of attacks, or giving general and vague "orders"  such as "attack this and gain root access" which makes them act like a toddler rather then a full stack hacker.

And because -as we said- the history of computer science is the history of good analogies, We should simulate the "real" battlefield and make up for "doctrines" for these agents.

Actually, writing a doctrine here make more sense because of the bounded predictable nature of this battlefield constituted of (bounded) computers, this is why we detailed that before. Doctrines will be more "efficient".
But what is different between this and writing a Guidlines.md or Agents.md or Skills.md ? Well, everything. 
The tone and language of the doctrine is different, which force it to adopt a "persona" (which a good practice) of a military operative. We can start by giving an example of how different they are by the "terrain analogy".

Humans conducted warfare on various places, forests, deserts, mountains, plains, urban areas, sea, air, tunnels...and each one has "a philosophy of fighting".
For instance, in the desert, the most important perk is speed. therefore the attackers must use of quick engines. the reconnaissance changes also, if a large unit is tasked with doing recon, it will be detected as there is no places where a group of people can hide.
Mapping the network and software architectures to these types **may** give the agents an edge to apply their technical deep "expertise" in a specific way. and who knows, maybe using real fighting tactics in the doctrines may be an idea in this even changing warfare landscape.

In my opinion, the intersection between the very large military heritage of strategies and tactics should be leveraged to better protect ourselves in this digital world (that is a mirror of the real one), as millions of lives were the price for such lessons, and they should not go to waste trying to reinvent the wheel of "how to defend and attack".
Will it yields good results ? or rather burden the agents ? I think it is worth exploring.
